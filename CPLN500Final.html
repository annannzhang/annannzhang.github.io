All-Purpose Tool & Double-edged Sword:

Transportation and Infrastructure Planning (TIP) in History

Upon the creation of DOT (Department of Transportation) in 1966 through legislation passed by Congress and its beginning of operation the following year, Transportation and Infrastructure Planning (TIP) started to gain more attention as an independent field of expertise under the larger field of urban planning in the most recent years. Nonetheless, tracing back into planning history, TIP seldom appeared to be the political priority or a guiding principle. Rather, it has played and is still playing a critical supporting role for enabling and realizing abstract political agendas for each time period. In most examples, however, TIP is deeply intertwined with other subfields under planning and becomes a double-edged sword which affects lives in cities.

This essay intends to unpack the intersectionality between TIP and the political priorities and planning fields throughout the four eras[1] in planning history –– 1) Sanitary Reforms from 1820s to 1920s centering around public health; 2) City Beautiful and Garden Cities movements in the first few decades of the 20th century led by urban designers and landscape architects; 3) post-war reconstruction that prioritized economic growth and efficiency into the 1990s under the influence of rational planning; and 4) focus on sustainable development on an international scale as we stepped into the new millennium. [Appendix: Table 1] Through examples across time and space, we will see how TIP served both as an all-purpose tool and a double-edged sword used by various entities and stakeholders, from architects and planners to colonial, federal, and local governmental officials, to realize their political ideals while the outcomes are not always ideal.

TIP x Public Health:

Sanitary Reform, Clearance & Cleansing (1820s – 1920s)

From mapping contagious diseases to spatial separation of patients from the healthy population, many early modern urban planning and municipal management strategies have their roots in the sanitary reform movement. Since the existing sanitary issues were mostly related to high urban density of both population and houses as well as insufficient urban infrastructure, redesigning roads and other infrastructure became one of the primary public health solutions. On the one hand, cities, especially European cities with old historic districts, were in need of wide roads to leave more open spaces for ventilation and traffic. On the other hand, greenery alongside streets helps mitigate pollution that was prevalent in urban areas.

As one of the most quintessential models, in Benjamin Ward Richardson’s famous book, Hygeia, a City of Health, he included specifications on road and infrastructure designs for his envisioned city, Hyegia:

 

The acreage of our model city allows room for three wide main streets or boulevards, … are thoroughly ventilated, and in the day are filled with sunlight. They are planted on each side of the pathways with trees, and in many places with shrubs and evergreens…. As yet wood pavement set in asphalt has been found the best. It is noiseless, cleanly, and durable. Tramways are nowhere permitted, the system of underground railways being found amply sufficient for all purposes.[2]

 

In addition, he proposed the stone pavements to have an incline tilt while the streets are higher in center and lower on the margins, potentially designed for drainage, which aligns with other more resilient infrastructure that he proposed.

            While boulevards are frequently associated with later City Beautiful movement, we see an early implication of wide roads and streets for public health benefits such as good ventilation. As Richardson’s Hygeia model initiated the sanitary reform across Europe, the idea of redesigning city’s road and transit systems for better health traveled far to many European colonies. For instance, the British found mid 19th-century Delhi slum-like, given “the dense and irregular pattern of Delhi’s streets and houses were reminiscent of the dire poverty and overcrowding of industrial slums in Britain.”[3] After several decades of British ruling, Delhi was considered a ‘healthy city’ by European observers after acquiring new wide roads and open space and installing electric lights and water and sewer systems,[4] and improved infrastructure signaled progress.[5] During this period, the rationale behind widening roads or larger-scale urban redesign remained functional and centered around public health. In the subsequent era, we see a shift towards more aesthetics and morality focus behind decisions of widening roads. In both eras, however, road and infrastructure design were heavily guided more by public health or urban design.

 

TIP x Design & Landscape:

City Beautiful & Garden Cities (1900s – 1940s)

While the sanitary reform and City Beautiful / Garden City are categorized into two eras in this paper, it is important to recognize that the two eras overlap and share some characteristics in actual transportation plans, such as the design of wide roads. In some examples, a set of actions were implemented to “kill two birds with one stone” — improve sanitation by population separation and embellish the cityscape with wide, modern boulevards that signals progress. This tends to be more prevalent in imperial colonies, since they were deemed “labs” for testing out different city planning and management strategies. For instance, in historian Gwendolyn Wright’s writing about Madagascar under French colonization, “a ring road of broad boulevards (completed in 1926, following Cassaigne’s new plan of that year) provided both a modern traffic artery and a cordon sanitaire, in this case isolating the city from its medical zone …for plague victims…Separation also referred to the “garden cities” on the outskirts of the capital.”[6]

            What separates the plans and projects that fall into the two eras are fundamentally the rationale and motivation behind similar design features. From Haussmann’s diagonal boulevards to Ebenezer Howard’s Garden Cities [Fig. 1] with rings of boulevards and railway systems separating residential and industrial areas[7], the projects in this era put more emphasis on the visual effects and their connection with people’s morals through the lens of urban design and landscape architecture, instead of focusing on public health aspects.

            Two prominent examples would be Burnham’s 1909 Chicago Plan [Fig. 2], inspired by the City Beautiful movement, and Batavia (Jakarta)’s planned community in Menteng under Dutch control, modeling Howard’s Garden City. Burnham’s Chicago Plan put emphasis on creating picturesque visual elements along the drive, including diagonally intersecting wide avenues radiating from a monument[8] and a 7.5 mile shore drive reached by seven viaducts and a lagoon, with shores “ornamented with trees and shrubs adapted to our climate.”[9] There is a similar element of wide cross-cutting boulevards and a central public square, and an elaborate monument at the northern entrance of the community in Dutch architect P.A.J. Mooijen’s original plan for the community in Batavia.[10]

City Beautiful and Garden City models stepped down upon the end of the second world war, as their monumentality was partially associated with dictatorship during the war and the world was moving towards a functional and rational paradigm to reconstruct the wounded land from bombing and other kinds of tragedies, when revitalizing economic and urban development became the priority.

 

TIP x Economic (Re)development & Renewal:

Post-war Reconstruction (1950s – 1990s)

            The post-war rise of highway, expressways, and arterial roads did not come from nowhere. Since the end of World War II, many nations had been seeking ways to recover, both economically and physically, from catastrophe, through an efficiency-driven rational paradigm for planning and producing policies. Highways or expressways then became a great manifestation of this paradigm – the acceleration of mobility of goods and human capitals produce a solid foundation for economic growth in a straightforward and rational approach. Under such a political agenda, the United States welcomed the birth of the influential National Interstate and Defense Highways Act of 1956 and the Federal-Aid Highway Act of 1956 can be attributed to President Eisenhower’s favor of highway networks from his earlier experience during World War II.[11] Subsequently, the Federal Highway Administration was created in the late 60s under the Department of Transportation and it supported many new expressway projects.[12]

            Benefiting from the increasing fundings and federal governmental support after the signing of the new Highway Acts, many projects that were proposed earlier were finally able to be pushed forward, including the regional highway system in Massachusetts which was proposed in 1948[13] and the Vine Street Expressway in Philadelphia [Fig. 3]. Indeed, just as suggested earlier, transportation planning is a double-edged sword — both the highway system in Massachusetts and Vine Street  Expressway, which cut through Philadelphia’s Chinatown and eventually left a huge ‘scar’ to the city, encountered huge push back from local communities.[14] Yet, the backlashes of constructing new highways again demonstrates how transportation planning in this era did not prioritize residents’ travel needs but rather anticipated economic development.

As the Vine Street Expressway plan started to come into shape carrying hope to bring in new blood to Philadelphia, on the other side of the planet, Tokyo, Japan was facing more pressing deadlines for an urban redevelopment as they were announced to be the host of 1964 Olympics in 1959, a critical international appearance since the end of World War II. As Sorensen summarized, Tokyo Government took Olympics as “an opportunity to leverage in national government funds to build some such needed urban infrastructure” – not only were there major road improvements but also “thirty routes totaling 138 kilometers in length were built,” many were for the Olympics.[15]

Though what we define in the current day as Transit-Oriented Development (TOD) was not yet a convention in transportation planning, we could identify the emergence of commercial activities near important transit hubs. From suburban shopping centers in the States to Shotenkai, roofed station-front shopping streets in Japan, we again see evidence of a booming post-war economy and vital commercial activities to which transportation planning was firmly tied to.

 

TIP x Sustainable & International Development (21st Century)

Upon the completion of the Millennium Summit in September 2000, the United Nations established eight Millennium Development Goals (MDGs), which were succeeded by Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) in 2015 as a part of Agenda 2030. The official establishment of SDGs and on-going global discussion on sustainable development marks the start of a new era of human development guided by sustainability. Although there is no SDG specifically dedicated to transportation, it is tightly connected with several generic development goals – Goal 7: Affordable and Clean Energy, Goal 9: Industry, Innovation, and Infrastructure, Goal 11 Sustainable Cities and Communities, and Goal 13 – Climate Actions.[16]

In concurrence, as the economic growth in Global South[17] started to take off, we see rapid urbanization in those developing countries in the first two decades of the 21st century. The urban settlements in the Global South have become the focal points for practicing sustainable planning, given their large scale and large share of Greenhouse Gas (GHG) emissions. Within sustainable planning, transportation is usually deemed the frontline target for mitigation[18] strategies, not because the government and planners see particular significance in transportation planning, but because it tends to produce more noticeable positive outcomes (e.g., how many tons of carbon emission are reduced by implementing a sustainable transportation system).

In Professor Erick Guerra’s article, “The Global South,” he outlined the challenges and case studies of transportation systems and infrastructure in the Global South, such as BRT (Bus Rapid Transit) in Guangzhou, China and Indonesia and the well-known Medellin Metrocable.[19] While Professor Guerra offered holistic reviews on transportation planning in Global South, he did not go into details about how many of those projects are funded by multilateral banks, which puts restraints on the design and construction. As large multilateral financing institutions such as the World Bank Group are committed to dedicate more funding into fighting against climate change, many transportation-related projects are approved for funding not only based on the local need for better transit systems but also based on how much the project can help mitigate GHG emission and fight climate change. The Quito Metro in Ecuador [Fig 4], for instance, received funding from European Investment Bank, World Bank, Inter-American Development bank and several other banks, partially because using the metro as replacement of fossil fuel powered public transport vehicles would largely help reduce carbon emissions.

Nonetheless, as all nations are further down the road of fighting climate change, transportation planners should be extra cautious on the socio-economic effect of switching to more sustainable transportation – for example, are most people able to afford transit systems that are more environmentally friendly but more expensive? Many more questions remain for planners to consider as we move from TIP (transportation and infrastructure planning) to STIP (sustainable transportation and infrastructure planning).

 

Conclusion

            There has been much controversy and criticism surrounding transportation planning, especially surrounding the rise of highways in the early 1960s and their adverse effect on local communities. However, as examples from four defined eras show, traffic has long been merely a political tool to achieve certain objectives or visions. The issues in TIP reflect lack of considerations in larger planning and governing, which requires deeper diagnosis rather than calling out one or to expressways or simply eradicating one means of transportation. With the professionalization of transportation planning as an independent subfield, transportation planners can better handle this all-purpose tool and double-edged sword to make sure no communities are disadvantaged.
